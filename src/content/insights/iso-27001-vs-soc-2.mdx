This is the question I get asked more than any other, usually in the first five minutes of a call: "Do we need ISO 27001, or is SOC 2 the one we're supposed to get?" Nine times out of ten, the business asking hasn't picked a framework - they've been told by a customer, an investor or a tender document that they need "a security certification," without anyone specifying which one. So let's fix that. Here's the plain-English version, without the sales pitch.

## What Is ISO 27001?

ISO 27001 is an international standard for information security management, published by the [International Organization for Standardization](https://www.iso.org/standard/27001). It sets out requirements for building an Information Security Management System (ISMS) - the policies, risk assessments, controls and records that show you're managing information security deliberately, not by accident.

The structure will look familiar if you've seen any other management system standard: clauses covering leadership, planning, risk treatment and continual improvement, backed by Annex A - a set of 93 security controls (access control, cryptography, supplier relationships, incident management and so on) that you select from based on your actual risk assessment, not blindly implement in full.

Certification is issued by an accredited certification body after a two-stage audit and it's valid for three years with annual surveillance audits in between. It's a genuine certificate - a document with a registration number that a customer, regulator or auditor can independently verify.

## What Is SOC 2?

SOC 2 (System and Organization Controls 2) is a reporting framework developed by the [American Institute of Certified Public Accountants (AICPA)](https://www.aicpa-cima.com/resources/landing/system-and-organization-controls-soc-suite-of-services). It assesses how well your controls meet the five "Trust Services Criteria" - security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality and privacy - though in practice almost every business only pursues the security criterion, with the others added if they're genuinely relevant.

The important distinction: SOC 2 doesn't produce a certificate. It produces an **attestation report**, written by a licensed CPA firm, describing your controls and - for a Type II report - testing whether they actually operated effectively over a defined period, usually six to twelve months. A Type I report is a point-in-time opinion; Type II is the one most B2B software buyers actually want, because it proves the controls held up over time, not just on audit day.

SOC 2 was built for the US market and is most common among SaaS and technology companies selling into it, but Australian and other international businesses pursue it constantly now, particularly if a chunk of their customer base is American.

## The Biggest Misconceptions

**"SOC 2 is easier than ISO 27001."** Not necessarily - it's *different*, not lighter. SOC 2 Type II in particular requires evidence that controls operated consistently for months, which can feel more demanding than a one-off ISO audit if your processes aren't already disciplined day-to-day.

**"You either pass or fail SOC 2."** You don't. The auditor's report documents what they found, including any exceptions. A report with a couple of noted exceptions still gets issued - it's not a pass/fail gate the way an ISO certification decision is.

**"They cover the same ground, so it doesn't matter which one you pick."** They overlap heavily on substance - access control, change management, incident response, vendor risk - but they answer a different question. ISO 27001 tells the market "our security management system meets an internationally recognised standard." SOC 2 tells a specific customer "here is independently verified evidence our controls actually worked over this period." Same underlying discipline, different currency.

**"Once you have one, the other is basically automatic."** There's real, useful overlap in the underlying controls - enough that going second is meaningfully faster than starting cold - but you still need a fresh gap assessment and audit for whichever framework you add.

## Which Businesses Should Choose ISO 27001?

In my experience, ISO 27001 is the better fit when:

- You sell primarily into Australia, the UK, Europe or Asia, where ISO certification is the more widely recognised reference point
- You're responding to government or enterprise tenders that specifically ask for "ISO 27001 certified" - a SOC 2 report often won't satisfy that box on the form
- You want a single security certification that sits alongside other ISO standards you already hold (ISO 9001, ISO 45001) under one integrated management system
- Your customer base is broad and international rather than concentrated in US enterprise software buyers - a certificate is easier to point to generically than a report written for a US audience
- You want the three-year certification cycle rather than refreshing an attestation report annually

## Which Businesses Should Choose SOC 2?

SOC 2 tends to be the right call when:

- You're a SaaS or technology company selling to US-based customers, particularly mid-market and enterprise software buyers - SOC 2 Type II is often a hard requirement in their vendor security questionnaire, not a nice-to-have
- Your sales cycle is being held up right now by prospects asking for "a SOC 2 report" by name
- You want to demonstrate operational maturity over time (Type II) rather than a point-in-time assessment
- You're moving fast and want to scope the engagement tightly to the Trust Services Criteria that actually matter to your customers, rather than a broader management system

## Can You Have Both?

Yes and plenty of growing tech businesses eventually do - usually ISO 27001 first for the broader market and tenders, then SOC 2 once US enterprise deals start showing up in the pipeline, or the other way around depending on which market opened up first.

The good news is the overlap does real work for you. Once you've built an ISMS for ISO 27001 - risk assessments, access control policies, incident response procedures, vendor management - a large share of that evidence maps directly onto SOC 2's control criteria. You're not starting from zero on the second framework; you're mostly re-presenting existing controls against a different set of criteria and adding whatever gaps the new framework introduces. I'd still budget for a proper gap assessment before assuming it's a light lift - "mostly the same" and "the same" are not the same thing when an auditor is involved.

## My Recommendation to Clients

I don't have a default answer here and I'd be suspicious of anyone who does - it genuinely depends on who's asking for it. The first question I ask every client is: **"Who is actually requesting this and what did they ask for by name?"** If it's a government tender or an Australian enterprise customer, that's usually ISO 27001. If it's a US software buyer's security questionnaire, that's usually SOC 2. If nobody's specifically asked yet and you're doing this proactively to open doors, I lean ISO 27001 for most Australian SMEs, simply because it's the more broadly recognised reference point outside the US and it plays well alongside standards like [ISO 9001](/iso-9001) if you're already running a quality system.

What I actively discourage is picking a framework because it sounds more impressive or because a competitor has it. Both frameworks are real work - proper risk assessments, real policies, evidence that gets checked. Spend that effort on the framework your actual customers are asking for.

## Key Takeaways

- ISO 27001 is a certificate from an accredited certification body; SOC 2 is an attestation report from a licensed CPA firm - they're structurally different, not just differently named.
- ISO 27001 tends to be the stronger fit for Australian, UK, European and government-facing work; SOC 2 tends to be the stronger fit for US enterprise SaaS sales.
- SOC 2 isn't automatically "easier" - Type II in particular demands months of consistent evidence, which can be harder than a one-off audit if your processes aren't already disciplined.
- The two frameworks overlap enough that holding one meaningfully speeds up getting the other, but neither is optional homework once you've done the first.
- The right starting point is almost always "what is our customer actually asking for," not "which one looks better on a website."
